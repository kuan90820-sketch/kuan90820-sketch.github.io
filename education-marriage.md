## Research Question

Does higher education expansion negatively affect marriage outcomes in Taiwan?

## Motivation

Higher education may increase socioeconomic status and income, which could raise the likelihood of marriage. At the same time, longer schooling and stronger career incentives may delay marriage or increase non-marriage. This project asks whether the observed change in marriage outcomes is related to higher education itself, rather than merely reflecting cohort-level delayed marriage.

## Method

The project uses survey microdata and applies regression-based analysis. The baseline model estimates the association between college education and ever-married status, then introduces controls such as age, gender, religion, parental education, and region/cohort fixed effects.

## Current Findings

A naive model suggests that college-educated respondents are less likely to have ever married. After controlling for age, the estimated coefficient becomes much smaller, indicating that age composition explains a substantial share of the raw association.

## Tools

R, tidyverse, fixest, modelsummary, regression analysis, fixed effects.
